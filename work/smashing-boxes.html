Case Study · 04 / 04

Building the system that makes the rest possible.

A year inside a product agency, learning the muscle that powers everything since: how to translate ambiguity into shippable systems, quickly, for clients who need both speed and consistency.

Company
Smashing Boxes
Role
UX/UI Designer
Timeline
2019-2020 · Durham, NC
Surface
Web · mobile · cross-client
Hero visual, system components
01
Context

Agency speed, system thinking.

Smashing Boxes is a product and software studio building web, mobile, and connected solutions for client teams that don't have their own. The challenge was the agency challenge: every project starts from zero, ships under a deadline, and has to look like the work of a senior team even when it's not the same team twice.

The lever was systems. If you build the right components once, every subsequent project gets faster and better.

02
What I did

Five patterns. Ten-plus projects.

  • Partnered with engineers to define 5 core interaction patterns and design system components, the kind that get reused across client projects to lift overall consistency.
  • Conducted qualitative user research across 10+ projects to ground product decisions in evidence rather than client preference.
  • Developed journey maps and interactive prototypes to align cross-functional teams on complex flows, especially valuable when the team rotates between projects.
  • Influenced product strategy through executive-ready storytelling: presentations that take a tangle of research and turn it into a single decision the room can make.
03
What it taught me

The agency drill.

Agency work compresses the design cycle in a way you can't get any other way. You learn to ask the question that unlocks the brief in the first conversation. You learn to build only what gets reused. You learn to ship without losing taste.

Everything I do as a senior designer now runs on muscle I built here.

Carry-forward

Whenever I start a new project today, the first instinct is the same: what's the smallest set of patterns that, if we get them right, will make every other decision faster?
